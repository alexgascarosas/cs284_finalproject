Summary

We propose a system for active view selection in 3D reconstruction, where an agent intelligently chooses camera viewpoints to improve reconstruction quality with fewer observations. Instead of random sampling, we will combine coverage-based exploration and uncertainty-based refinement to guide the view selection process. We will evaluate our approach against baseline methods to show improved reconstruction efficiency.

Problem description

Multi-view 3D reconstruction is fundamentally dependent on the quality and diversity of input viewpoints. Conventional pipelines assume either dense or randomly sampled observations, which can lead to redundant coverage of already-observed regions while failing to capture occluded or geometrically complex areas. In practical settings such as robotics and embodied vision, observations are costly, and thus the problem of selecting informative viewpoints becomes critical.

This problem is commonly framed as a Next Best View (NBV) task, where the objective is to select the next camera pose that maximizes expected improvement in reconstruction quality. However, existing approaches often emphasize a single criterion—either maximizing coverage of unseen regions or reducing uncertainty in poorly reconstructed areas.

In this project, we investigate whether combining these complementary objectives can yield more efficient reconstruction. Specifically, we model an iterative pipeline in which an agent: (1) acquires an observation from a selected viewpoint, (2) updates a partial 3D reconstruction, (3) evaluates candidate viewpoints based on a scoring function, and (4) selects the next viewpoint to maximize reconstruction improvement. Our approach introduces a hybrid scoring function that balances exploration (coverage) and refinement (uncertainty), providing a principled yet computationally tractable alternative to learning-based NBV methods.

Goals and deliverables

Research questions

  • Does a hybrid NBV criterion (coverage + uncertainty) improve reconstruction quality per observation compared to using either criterion alone?
  • How much more efficient is guided view selection than random viewpoint sampling, as a function of the number of views?
  • Which components of the pipeline (rendering, fusion, scoring) dominate runtime, and where can we simplify without losing too much quality?

(1) Baseline plan — must deliver

End-to-end loop: scene → image acquisition → reconstruction → view selection → iteration.

Rendering and data generation

We will simulate image acquisition from known camera poses using synthetic scenes. This allows controlled evaluation of different view selection strategies.

3D reconstruction

We will implement a classical reconstruction pipeline (voxel-based or TSDF fusion) using tools such as Open3D. Output will be a point cloud or mesh representation that is incrementally updated after each new view.

View selection strategies

We will implement and compare the following:

  • Random sampling (baseline)
  • Coverage-based selection (maximizing visibility of previously unseen regions)
  • Uncertainty-based selection (targeting poorly reconstructed regions)
  • Hybrid selection (ours — combining coverage and uncertainty)

Evaluation and metrics

We will evaluate reconstruction quality as a function of the number of views. Metrics will include:

  1. Geometric accuracy (e.g. Chamfer distance to a reference shape when available)
  2. Qualitative visual comparisons
  3. Efficiency (quality improvement per observation)

Visuals and results

We will produce:

  • Progressive reconstructions over time
  • Side-by-side comparisons across methods
  • Visualizations of camera trajectories and selected viewpoints

(2) Aspirational plan — hope to deliver

If we move ahead of schedule, we hope to explore extensions inspired by neural radiance fields (NeRFs), more complex scenes or real-world datasets, and alternative uncertainty estimation strategies.
